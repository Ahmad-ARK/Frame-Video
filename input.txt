VERBATIM: In 1885, European powers met in Berlin and carved up Africa without a single African in the room. The largest single prize went to one man personally — King Leopold II of Belgium, who took a territory eighty times the size of his own country and called it the Congo Free State. It was neither free nor a state. It was a rubber plantation run on severed hands, and by the time the world forced Belgium to take it over in 1908, millions were dead.

So today we're going to trace three timelines over one piece of land. The colonizer's timeline. The Cold War superpower's timeline. And the Congolese timeline underneath both. Then we'll ask the only question that matters: after a century and a half, who actually walked away with the wealth.

The Congo is not poor. It is one of the richest patches of ground on earth — copper, gold, diamonds, and later the minerals the modern world cannot function without: cobalt and coltan. That is the whole tragedy in one sentence. The Congo has never been exploited because it was weak and worthless. It has been fought over precisely because it was strong and priceless.
Leopold's private empire ran on forced rubber collection. Quotas were enforced by cutting off the hands of workers, including children, who failed to meet them. Estimates of the dead over two decades run to the millions. When photographs of the mutilations reached Europe, the scandal forced Belgium to seize the colony from its own king in 1908.

For the next fifty years it was simply the Belgian Congo — governed for the benefit of Brussels, its copper and uranium flowing out. The uranium in the bombs dropped on Hiroshima and Nagasaki was mined here.
Then in 1960, independence came fast and unprepared. The first democratically elected prime minister was a thirty-four-year-old named Patrice Lumumba, who made one fatal choice: he spoke about controlling Congo's own resources, and when the West hesitated, he accepted Soviet help.

In the logic of 1960, that made him a target. Within months, Lumumba was removed in a crisis backed by Belgium and the United States, handed to his enemies, and executed. Declassified records later confirmed the CIA had been authorized to assassinate him; he was killed before their own plot was carried out.
Into the vacuum stepped an army officer named Mobutu Sese Seko. He would rule for thirty-two years. He was corrupt, brutal, and absolutely reliable as an anti-communist ally — which is exactly why Washington and Brussels backed him for three decades while he looted the country he governed.

Mobutu renamed the country Zaire and renamed himself in a title that translated to "the all-powerful warrior who goes from conquest to conquest." He stole an estimated five billion dollars while roads, hospitals, and schools collapsed. For the entire Cold War this was acceptable, because a stable thief who hated Moscow was worth more to the West than an unpredictable reformer who did not.
Then in 1991 the Soviet Union collapsed, and Mobutu's only value — being anti-communist — collapsed with it. Suddenly the West had no use for him. Within six years he was gone.

What followed was not peace. It was the deadliest conflict since the Second World War, and almost no one in the West can name it. The First and Second Congo Wars, from 1996 into the 2000s, pulled in nine African nations fighting over Congolese territory and minerals. Somewhere between three and five million people died, mostly from disease and starvation as the state disintegrated.
And the fighting never fully stopped. Eastern Congo remains a war zone today, where armed groups and neighboring countries fund themselves by controlling mines — the same cobalt and coltan that end up in the phone in your hand and the battery in electric cars.

Here is the part worth sitting with. Ask who won across a hundred and forty years.
Belgium built a modern European nation on Congolese rubber and copper, then walked away from the wreckage. The United States got a reliable Cold War asset and the uranium for its first atomic bombs, then lost interest the moment the Cold War ended. A century of outside powers extracted staggering wealth and left almost none of it behind.
And the Congolese? They sit on top of the resources powering the twenty-first century, and remain among the poorest people on earth, in a country that has been looted continuously since before their great-grandparents were born.

This isn't a story about a cursed country. It's a story about a mechanism: when a land is rich enough, outside powers will always find a reason — civilization, anti-communism, security, supply chains — to control it, install someone who serves them, and call the extraction something other than what it is. The names on the outside changed every few decades. The direction the wealth traveled never did.
