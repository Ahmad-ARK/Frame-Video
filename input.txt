VERBATIM: In 1953, the CIA helped overthrow Iran's elected government to install a Shah loyal to the West. In 1979, Iranians overthrew that Shah and took fifty-two Americans hostage for four hundred and forty-four days. Since then, no sitting American president has visited Tehran, and no sitting Iranian president has visited Washington. Israel has never recognized Iran's government. Iran has never recognized Israel's right to exist. For over forty years this has produced a war that almost nobody can name the start date of, because it never had one.

So today we're going to trace three timelines at once. The American timeline. The Israeli timeline. And the Iranian timeline. Then we're going to test whether any of the three actually won anything, against what happened in the decades that followed.

The 1979 revolution didn't just remove a monarchy. It installed a government built explicitly around opposing the United States and, by extension, its closest Middle Eastern ally. Ayatollah Khomeini called America "the Great Satan." He called Israel "the Little Satan." Those weren't throwaway insults. They became the organizing principle of Iranian foreign policy for the next four decades.
Within a year, Iraq invaded Iran. The United States quietly backed Saddam Hussein, supplying intelligence and, later, tacit cover for chemical weapons use against Iranian troops. Half a million people died. Iran emerged from that war isolated, embittered, and convinced that Western powers would always tilt against it.
It was during this period that Iran built its clearest strategic doctrine: never fight a war on your own soil again. Build proxies instead.

That doctrine had a name: the "Axis of Resistance." Hezbollah in Lebanon, founded in 1982 with direct Iranian funding and training. Later, support flowed to militias in Iraq, to Palestinian groups, to the Houthis in Yemen. Each proxy gave Iran reach without direct exposure — the same logic Pakistan used arming the mujahideen, the same logic America used funding them.
Israel watched this doctrine build for twenty years with a singular fear: a nuclear-armed Iran, fighting through proxies it couldn't always trace back to Tehran.

By the 2000s, that fear had a specific target. Iran's nuclear program, hidden for years, was exposed in 2002 by an opposition group. Enrichment facilities at Natanz and Fordow became the centers of a shadow war that never got declared, only discovered afterward.
In 2010, a computer worm called Stuxnet — built jointly, reporting later confirmed, by American and Israeli intelligence — physically destroyed a fifth of Iran's centrifuges without firing a shot. Between 2010 and 2012, four Iranian nuclear scientists were assassinated on the streets of Tehran. Iran blamed Israel. Israel never officially confirmed or denied it.
This was the shape the conflict took for a decade: sabotage, assassination, deniability. A war fought so quietly that most of the world only learned about each battle after it was already over.

In 2015, a different approach: the Iran nuclear deal. The United States, alongside five other world powers, agreed to lift sanctions in exchange for strict limits on enrichment, verified by international inspectors. Iran's economy began to reopen. For three years, the shadow war paused.
Then in 2018, the United States withdrew from the agreement unilaterally and reimposed sanctions. Iran's economy collapsed back into crisis. The pause ended.
In January 2020, an American drone strike killed Qasem Soleimani, the general who had run Iran's entire regional proxy network for two decades, as he left Baghdad airport. It was the most direct blow either side had landed on the other in the war's entire history. Iran responded with a missile barrage on American bases in Iraq. No one on either side wanted the exchange to escalate further. It didn't — that time.

For the next few years, the shadow war intensified without ever fully surfacing: explosions at Natanz, ships damaged in the Persian Gulf, cyberattacks traded in both directions. Then on October 7, 2023, Hamas — a group Iran had funded and armed for years — launched an attack into southern Israel that killed over a thousand people. Israel's response in Gaza opened the deadliest chapter of the conflict yet, and Iran's other proxies, Hezbollah and the Houthis, opened fronts of their own in solidarity.
In April 2024, for the first time in the conflict's history, Iran launched a direct missile and drone attack on Israeli territory from its own soil, rather than through a proxy. Israel struck back directly in return. The deniability that had defined this war for forty years was gone. Both sides had now attacked each other openly, admitted it, and dared the other to respond.

Here is the part worth sitting with. Ask who actually won any of this.
America removed a hostile government in 1953 and got a far more hostile one in 1979 that has outlasted six American presidents. It fought Iran by proxy through Iraq in the 1980s and ended up invading that same Iraq twice. It walked away from a working nuclear agreement in 2018 and got a nuclear program with fewer restraints than before.
Israel dismantled Iran's nuclear scientists and centrifuges for a decade and still faces a program that keeps rebuilding. It degraded Hezbollah and Hamas repeatedly since the 1980s and still fights both today.
Iran built proxies specifically so it would never again fight a war on its own territory, and in 2024 found itself trading direct missile fire with Israel anyway — the exact outcome forty years of doctrine was built to avoid.
Every side that thought it was managing this conflict from a safe distance eventually found the distance had closed.

This isn't a story about who is right. It's a story about a mechanism: proxies escalate until deniability runs out, and deniability always eventually runs out. Three governments have spent forty-five years insisting they were the ones in control of the timeline. None of them were. The timeline was in control of them.
