VERBATIM: November 24th, 1971. Thanksgiving eve. A man in a dark suit and sunglasses walks up to the counter at Portland International Airport and buys a one-way ticket under the name Dan Cooper. Cash. No luggage. Nothing about him stands out. Twenty minutes into the flight, he hands a note to a flight attendant. She assumes it's a phone number. He leans in and tells her to read it now. It says he has a bomb in his briefcase.

He opens the case just enough for her to see wires and red cylinders, then closes it. His demands are specific and calm: two hundred thousand dollars in cash, four parachutes, and a fuel truck waiting when they land in Seattle. No theatrics. No shouting. He orders a bourbon and soda while they wait, and pays for it.

The airline gives him everything he asks for. In Seattle, the plane sits on a dark runway while the money — ten thousand twenty-dollar bills, later revealed to have been secretly photographed and serial-numbered by the FBI — is loaded aboard along with the parachutes. He releases all thirty-six passengers and two flight attendants. Only the pilots, one attendant, and Cooper remain. He orders the plane back into the air, heading toward Mexico City at a specific low altitude and slow speed he seems to know precisely how to request. Somewhere over the dense, freezing forest of southwest Washington, he lowers the aft staircase of the Boeing 727 — the only commercial jet ever built with stairs that fold out from the tail in flight — and steps out into a rainstorm, at night, at ten thousand feet, wearing nothing but a suit and a parachute.

He is never seen again.

What follows is one of the largest manhunts in FBI history. Agents and soldiers search hundreds of square miles of Pacific Northwest wilderness for weeks. They find nothing — no body, no parachute, no briefcase. For eight years, the money vanishes as completely as the man. Then in February 1980, an eight-year-old boy digging in the sand along the Columbia River finds three decaying bundles of cash, still bound by rubber bands, totaling five thousand eight hundred dollars. The serial numbers match Cooper's ransom exactly.
And that discovery answers almost nothing. The bills are badly deteriorated, consistent with years underwater, but investigators can't agree on how they got there — washed downstream from where he landed, buried deliberately, or dropped by someone who never froze to death in that forest at all.

For decades, the FBI chased suspects. A man named Richard McCoy Jr. pulled off a nearly identical hijacking four months later and was caught within days — but his alibi for the Cooper flight held up, and he didn't match witness descriptions closely enough. A flight attendant who worked the Cooper flight later said a former paratrooper named Kenneth Christiansen bore an uncanny resemblance on his deathbed confession — but no physical evidence ever tied him to the case. A convicted con man named Duane Weber allegedly told his wife his real name was "Dan" hours before he died — but DNA testing on partial genetic material recovered from the original necktie Cooper left behind excluded him. Every serious suspect named across fifty years has been circumstantially compelling and forensically inconclusive.
In 2016, after more than forty-five years and thousands of leads, the FBI formally suspended active investigation of the case. It remains the only unsolved commercial airplane hijacking in American history.

No confirmed trace of the man has ever surfaced. No body. No parachute. No definitive match to any name. Either he died within minutes of jumping, swallowed by a forest that has never given him back — or he planned an escape precise enough that a man in a business suit walked out of a storm at night and simply became someone else, forever, somewhere just outside the story anyone has ever been able to prove.
